What Makes CTF Different

CTF is a browser-first anime-and-video-game crossover card battle built around three active victory paths, a locked seven-phase turn structure, reactive chains, Libra summons, fusion power plays, and a living custom-card forge. It is not trying to feel like a museum piece. It feels like a game still hot enough to shape.

The site already includes the front page, rules page, glossary, cards browser, deck builder, duel page, checklist page, and custom-card submit page. You can run it locally with no framework pipeline, and it is shaped for GitHub Pages with a manifest and service worker so it behaves more like a lightweight app shell than a plain static website.

Online play is designed around room codes with PeerJS/WebRTC. The duel flow supports deck selection, room creation, joining by code, Hot-Seat mode, Solo Sandbox mode, reconnect scaffolding, rematches, result-card exports, duel logs, and post-match summaries. That means the project is not just a card database. It is already trying to be a usable game product.

Card Families

The live data shows six cardType values, but the cleanest way to understand CTF is as five main families plus one faster branch. The main families are Catalyst, Palm Trick, Concealed Trick, Field Trick, and Fusion. Counter Trick is the highest-speed branch of the Concealed side.

Catalysts

Catalysts are the bodies on the field. They attack, defend, block lanes, serve as tribute fuel, become Libra scales, get captured, and push all three win paths.

Palm Tricks

Palm Tricks are proactive and strictly your-turn cards. They do not jump into chains as responses. They are the move that starts the fire, not the shield thrown after the spark is already flying.

Concealed & Counter Tricks

Concealed Tricks are reactive traps. Counter Tricks are the sharper speed lane. Once a Counter Trick enters the chain, only another Counter Trick can answer it.

Field Tricks & Fusion

Field Tricks reshape the battlefield. Fusion cards live in the Fusion Deck and come out through exact material logic. The fusion audit showing 44 Fusion cards matched to 44 engine specs is a strong sign that this lane is already real, not cosmetic.

CTF also tries hard to speak its own language. Monster became Catalyst. Spell became Palm Trick. Trap became Concealed Trick. Life Points became Chi. Graveyard became Void. ATK and DEF became Pressure and Counter Pressure.

How You Win

Carry The Flame runs three victory paths at the same time: Chi KO, 7 Kills, and 7 Extractions. Chi starts at 10,000 in the live engine. If Chi hits zero, that win takes priority. After that, the game checks 7 Kills, then 7 Extractions.

Chi KO

Push direct damage and battle damage until the opponent’s Chi drops to zero.

7 Kills

Battle destruction matters. This path tracks combat kills, not every random destroy effect.

7 Extractions

Beat defending non-Great Catalysts, capture them into your Box, then convert them into extraction progress later.

Deck-out rule

The live build does not auto-lose on deck-out. If you cannot draw, the engine skips the draw and the duel continues.

The turn structure is hard-locked to seven phases: Turn Start, Draw, Ignition, Action, Battle, Resolution, and End. The first player still draws on turn one, but that player’s first Battle Phase is skipped so the duel gets a chance to breathe before early pressure starts.

Battle is also where CTF feels different. ATK vs ATK is straightforward: the bigger Pressure wins, the loser goes to the Void, Chi damage is dealt by the difference, and a Kill is counted. ATK vs DEF changes everything. A normal defender that loses gets captured into the Box instead of just dying. That is the lane that feeds Extractions.

Special Systems That Make CTF Feel Different

Shotgun Rule

Every Special Summon gives the opponent a draw in the live Patch 42 engine. That puts a real tax on explosive turns. You can still pop off, but the other player gets ammunition while you do it.

Libra

Libra Summon can bring out up to five Catalysts at once as long as their Levels fall strictly between the two scale values. The whole Libra Summon only counts as one Special Summon toward the hard cap.

Great Cards

Great Cards are powerful and heavily protected from Box abuse. Beat one in defense and it goes to the Void instead of being captured. Great Cards also cannot be used as End Phase sacrifices in the current build.

Ritual Clean-Up

Patch 42 removes the Ritual group from live card data and rewrites those legacy cards into cleaner Special Summon wording. That cleanup matters because it sharpens CTF’s own identity instead of dragging old imported system clutter around.

The Custom Card Forge

This is the feature younger players are going to lock onto immediately. Patch 42 already ships a live submission page where a player can design a five-card mini-set. That set includes three Normal Catalysts, one Great Fusion Catalyst, and one Trick Card.

The forge is not chaos. It uses rules. Players split a shared stat budget across their non-Fusion Catalysts, obey level caps and stat caps, build one exact fusion out of two chosen materials, and write clean text for the Trick and Fusion pieces. Then the page exports JSON and opens an email handoff for review.

The message is simple: CTF is not only saying “play the game.” It is saying “step into authorship.”

What The Code Is Really Doing

The engine is not a toy script that only flips cards and hopes for the best. It builds player state, opening hands, turn and phase tracking, chain state, pending responses, discard windows, per-turn usage tracking, summon context, duel logs, match summaries, and peer-to-peer sync data.

The duel UI handles room flow, deck selection, sandbox starts, Hot-Seat starts, choice modals, attack confirmation, tribute picking, End Phase choices, chain summaries, result cards, and log downloads. The deck layer handles save normalization, validation, and launch prep. The service worker and manifest keep the shell feeling fast and app-like.

What Is Finished — And What Is Still Being Forged

Patch 42 already has real strength. The shell is playable. The site structure is real. The deck tools exist. The room-code flow exists. The phase system is hard-locked. The battle rules are coded. Libra is coded. Fusion is coded. Great-card routing is tested. Starter deck effect coverage is strong. The custom-card forge is live. The visual identity now carries its own ember look.

At the same time, the project is not done. The audit still shows that most of the card pool needs dedicated custom scripting. Multiplayer needs broader live-device testing. Mobile polish and accessibility still need more passes. Some data tags still need normalization. That honesty helps the game more than pretending it is finished.

CTF feels like the kind of project people can still learn, play, argue about, improve, and maybe even leave their own card inside.

That is the real heat of the project. Not just collecting cards. Not just copying old systems. Not just watching somebody else’s universe. Carry The Flame wants players to step into the arena, step into the forge, and then step into authorship.
